How have rent, household income, and subway access co-evolved across New York City's boroughs?

New York's affordability story is gradual and measurable. This study tracks median rent, income, and transit access across five boroughs (2010‚Äì2024) to understand when and where affordability decoupled ‚Äî and why. We pair ACS, NYC Open Data, and MTA GTFS feeds to surface that the borough rent spread widened by since 2010.

Context: Why affordability pressure persists

The post-recession rebound and pandemic whiplash left New Yorkers chasing rent increases that rarely paused. Borough medians marched upward even when wage growth cooled, especially in transit-rich neighborhoods that stayed in demand.

    Limitations & future work

    • Borough aggregates mask intra-borough heterogeneity and building-level variation.
    • Income estimates lag real-time shocks; correlations may understate short-term rent swings.
    • Transit index excludes bus-only corridors and commuter rail accessibility.
    • Zoning changes, new housing permits, and migration flows were out of scope but merit follow-up.

    Reflection

    This work reinforced the value of choosing signal over spectacle: fewer, clearer charts, stronger definitions, and an evidence trail that survives outside the notebooks that produced it. Balancing statistical rigor with narrative clarity made the findings legible to policy readers and fellow analysts alike.
